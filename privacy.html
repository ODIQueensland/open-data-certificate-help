How do you protect people's privacy?

In the Privacy section of the Open Data Certificate questionnaire, you answer questions about the data you have published. Using your answers, further questions are asked based on this flowchart.
  1. Is this data about people or their activities?
  2. Has the data about individuals been de-identified?
  3. Has your de-identification process been independently audited?
  4. What permission do you have to publish this personal data online?
  5. Where do you document your right to publish this personal data?
  6. Have you assessed the privacy risks of publishing this data?
  7. Has your privacy risk assessment been independently audited?
  8. Where is your risk assessment published?
  9. Are you prepared to respond if publishing this data compromises the privacy of individuals?
Privacy Laws
In Australia, privacy laws do not bind everyone. For example:
  • the Commonwealth Privacy Act 1988 binds Commonwealth Government departments and businesses with a turnover greater than $3 million and private health service providers.
  • the Queensland Information Privacy Act 2009 binds Queensland Government Ministers, Departments, Local Governments, public authorities, and their bound contracted service providers. Not all areas of government are bound, for example, Government Owned Corporations, courts or tribunals in relation to their judicial or quasi-judicial functions, among other exemptions.
Some sectors, particularly health services, may be bound by more than one privacy law. The Australian Government provides a list of links to privacy regulation in the States and Territories and other Australian laws that relate to privacy.

 

Sensitive Data
The Open Data Certificate questionnaire focuses on privacy, one element of data sensitivity. Other elements of data sensitivity include:
  • commercial-in-confidence
  • secrecy
  • cultural heritage
  • environmental security.
It is important to consider all aspects of data sensitivity when publishing data. These resources may assist you to address other elements of data sensitivity:
  • Sensitive Information by the Australian Law Reform Commission
  • eHealth Security and Authentication by the National E-Health Transition Authority (NEHTA)
  • Ethics, consent and data sharing by the Australian National Data Service (ANDS).

Reference the Law
To help people use the data with confidence, in the data description or documentation provide a reference to the law that permits the data publication. Include:
  • the name of the law
  • a reference to the clause in the law that permits the data publication
  • a hyperlink to the law or clause within the law.
Example reference
The xyz data is required to be published by the abc Act 1999 as stated in clause 1.2.3.

Private Breach Resources
If the publishing of your dataset online results in the identity of the individuals whose data is contained in the dataset being reasonably ascertainable - you have potentially breached the privacy of these individuals. In the event that this comes to your attention, there are a number of steps that can be take to remedy a privacy breach. Guides to help you plan for a possible privacy breach:
  • Privacy breach management and notification by the Queensland Office of the Information Commissioner.
  • Data breach notification — A guide to handling personal information security breaches by the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner.
