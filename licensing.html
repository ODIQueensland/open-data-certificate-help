How do you give people permission to use this data?

In the Licensing section of the Open Data Certificate questionnaire, you may answer one or more questions that are progressively shown based on this flowchart.
  1. Where have you published the rights statement for this dataset?
  2. Is there copyright in this data?
  3. Is the data marked as Public Domain?
  4. Where are the rights and licencing of third-party data explained?
  5. Under which licence or waiver can people reuse this data?
  6. What is the name of the licence?
  7. Where is the licence?
  8. Is the licence an open licence?
  9. Do you have a machine-readable rights statement?

1. Where have you published the rights statement for this dataset?

Give the URL to a page that describes the right to re-use this dataset. This should include a reference to its license or copyright waiver, attribution requirements, and a statement about relevant copyright. A rights statement helps people understand what they can and can't do with the data.

Copyright URL

You should publish a rights statement that details copyright, licensing and how people should give attribution to the data.
Example
If your data is published on a web page, consider using the Creative Commons Licence Chooser or the Public Domain Mark Chooser to generate a rights statement and place it on the web page where your data can be accessed. These tools can generate both a human‑readable and machine‑readable rights statement. E.g.
Marine oil spills data by Transport and Main Roads is licensed under a Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International License.

2. Is there any copyright in the data?

No, the data only contains facts and numbers, or copyright has expired

There is no copyright in factual information. If the data does not contain any content that was created through intellectual effort, there are no rights in the content.
Note
Copyright only applies to data if you spent intellectual effort creating what's in it, for example, by writing text that's within the data, or deciding whether particular data is included. There's no copyright if the data only contains facts where no judgements were made about whether to include them or not.
Copyright vests (is considered to exist) immediately upon creation of a work, and expires 70 years after the death of the author. (Crown copyright has a different duration). Source: Copyright, Data and Licensing by Australian National Data Service licensed under CC BY 3.0 AU.

Yes, and the rights are held by different people or organisations

In some data, the rights in different records are held by different people or organisations. Information about rights needs to be kept in the data too.
Note
In some data, the rights in different records are held by different people or organisations. Where these nested rights carry different restrictions, the dataset will inherit, in total, the most restrictive of the nested restrictions. For example, if two nested elements of data are licensed CC-BY and CC-BY-NC, the entire dataset will need to be licensed CC-BY-NC, which by definition would prevent it from being ‘open’ data. There may be an exception to this rule if the user can clearly identify the nested data elements and the restrictions that apply to them. (To do: Provide an example how)

3. Is the data marked as Public Domain?

Works in the public domain are those whose intellectual property rights have expired, have been forfeited, or are inapplicable. If none of the content of your data have copyright or other rights over it, it is good practice to explicitly state this by labelling it as ‘public domain’. You can do this by using the Creative Commons Public Domain Mark or a no known rights statement.

5. Under which licence or waiver can people reuse this data?

Remember that whoever spends intellectual effort creating content automatically gets rights over it. Creative content includes the organisation and selection of items within data, but does not include facts. You need to provide a waiver or a licence which informs people how they can legally use the data. We list the most common licenses used in Australia.

Creative Commons Attribution

CC0

https://creativecommons.org/publicdomain/zero/1.0/
Note
The CC0 is a document in which the user purports to abandon all claim to copyright and related rights. It is not a licence. Whilst you could apply a CC0 Waiver, there are some doubts about its force under Australian law, particularly with respect to moral rights. Furthermore, the disclaimer that accompanies CC0, at present, may be ineffective in protecting the user from liability for claims of negligence. Source: Research Data FAQs by AusGOAL licensed under CC BY 3.0 AU.
