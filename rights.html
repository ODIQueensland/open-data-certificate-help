Do you have the legal right to share this data with people?

In the Legal Rights section of the Open Data Certificate questionnaire, you may answer one or more questions that are progressively shown based on this flowchart.
  1. Do you have the rights to publish this data as open data?
  2. Was all this data originally created or gathered by you? (If answer to question 1 was, "Yes")
  3. Was all this data originally created or gathered by you? (If answer to question 1 was, "Unsure")
  4. Where do you detail the risks people might encounter if they use this data?
  5. Was some of this data extracted or calculated from other data?
  6. Are all sources of this data already published as open data?
  7. Was some of this data crowdsourced?
  8. Did contributors to this data use their judgement?
  9. Where is the contributor licence or waiver?
  10. Have all contributors agreed to the contributor licence or waiver?
  11. Where do you describe sources of the third-party data?
  12. Is documentation about the sources of this data in machine-readable format?
 

1. Do you have the rights to publish this data as open data?

You should have a clear legal right to publish this data. If your organisation didn't originally create or gather this data then you might not have the right to publish it. If you’re not sure, check with the rights holder (owner) because you will need their permission to publish it.
Your dataset might contain data that has been provided by a third party. If you’re not sure whether you are the rights holder (owner) of the data, look for information about how your dataset was created. There may be owners whose permission you need to publish the data. If your data includes data from other sources, the owner of the data must give you permission to publish it. They could do this in several ways, including by:
  • applying an open licence to the data
  • providing permission to publish the data under an open licence
  • waiving their copyright in the data, thereby placing it in the public domain.
If the copyright in the data has expired, you don't need permission to publish it.

2. Was all this data originally created or gathered by you?

If any part of this data was sourced outside your organisation by other individuals or organisations, then you need to give extra information about your right to publish it.
If your data includes data from other sources, you can publish it if the copyright has expired or each rights holder of the third-party data has given you permission to publish it, by either:
  • applying an open licence to the data
  • providing you written permission to publish the data under an open licence
  • waiving their copyright in the data, thereby placing it in the public domain.

8. Did contributors to this data use their judgement?

If people used their skill, creativity or judgement to contribute data, then copyright may subsist in the data. For example, their judgement, skill or creativity may be writing a description or selecting a subset of data to include in their contribution. If so, in order for the contributed data to published as open data, contributors should transfer or waive their rights, or licence their contribution under an open licence before you publish or re-use it as open data.

9. Where is the contributors licence or waiver?

Provide a link to a licence or waiver that the contributors have adopted for their contribution to the data. A contributors licence or waiver will either:
  • transfer contributor's rights to you
  • waive their rights; or
  • licence the data to you so you can publish it.
Example
When people contribute to Australia's Regional Open Data Census, beside the Submit button is the statement, "By submitting material to the census you agreeing to the terms of use and also to license your contribution (to the extent there are any rights in it) under the Open Data Commons Public Domain Dedication and License." With this agreement in place, all crowd-sourced contributions are shown on the site and published as open data under the Open Data Commons Public Domain Dedication and License.

11. Where do you describe sources of the third-party data?

Give a URL that documents where the data was sourced from and the rights under which you publish the data. This helps people understand where the data comes from.
On the web page, for each dataset sourced from a third-party, include:
  • the title of the dataset used
  • the data publisher
  • the licence you use the data under
  • the attribution in the form the publisher has requested
  • links to the data, publisher and licence
  • if you made any changes to the third-party data
Example
This Wedding Planner dataset includes the following third-party data:
  • Noosa Wedding Location data by Noosa Shire Council used under a Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 Australia licence. Some location data has been adjusted to align with the aerial imagery base map.
  • Mooloolaba tide gauge—archived interval recordings by Transport and Main Roads used under a Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 Australia licence.
Note: In this case, even though the Noosa Shire Council waived the requirements of attribution, it is still helpful to document the sources of your data so data re-users are fully informed and can make their own judgements about reusing this data.
